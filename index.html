Step 1

A maze is just rooms and doors.

Imagine graph paper. Each square is a room. A wall is just a closed door between two neighboring rooms. Making a maze means deciding which doors to open.

The computer stores each square with four yes/no wall switches: north, east, south, and west. If the east wall opens in one square, the west wall must open in the square next to it.

Step 2

The maze algorithm: explore, carve, back up.

This project uses depth-first search. That sounds intense, but the rule is simple: walk to a neighbor you have never visited, knock down the wall, and remember your path with a stack. If you get stuck, step backward until a new choice appears.

  1. Start somewhere. Mark that square visited and put it on the stack.
  2. Look around. Find neighbors that are inside the grid and not visited yet.
  3. Choose one. Open the wall between the current square and that neighbor.
  4. Move forward. Mark the neighbor visited and push it onto the stack.
  5. Backtrack. If there are no choices, pop the stack and try from the earlier square.

Step 3

The math is mostly counting and coordinates.

A grid position is written as (x, y). Moving east adds 1 to x. Moving south adds 1 to y. That is how arrow keys become movement.

A perfect maze with N rooms has exactly N - 1 open doors. Why? Because every new door connects one brand-new room. For a 6 x 6 maze, that means 36 rooms and 35 carved doors.

Step 5

How the player and timer fit in.

Once the maze exists, the game loop repeats many times per second. It reads keyboard input, asks if movement is allowed, updates the timer, and draws the new picture on the canvas.
